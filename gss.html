    Note: I would like to clarify that there was an edit in this version of the powerpoint. I recall that during the session a student had asked whether histidine would be protonated at a pH of 7.4, to which I (incorrectly) said that it would be protonated. This is wrong. Just running through the calculations, given that histidine's R group pKa is 6, we see that the physiological pH of 7.4 would result in histidine being found in the deprotonated form nearly 25 times more than the protonated form. Thus, both N' - HAWAII - C' and N' - MICHIGAN - C' should have a net charge of 0 at physiological pH. Sorry for any confusion caused by this.

  • 09/14/2022 (Wed): Secondary Protein Structure - Virtual
    • Slides
    • Side chain interaction chart

    The student-made amino acid sequence N' - GAWIQVLNH - C' is a good example of side chain interactions between n and n+3, n+4 groups. The glycine at the first residue for instance could interact with the isoleucine at the fourth residue through induced dipole-induced dipole interactions. Similarly, the glutamine residue at residue number 5 can interact with the asparagine residue at residue 8 through hydrogen bonding interactions.

    I have also made a correction in the chart of interacting amino acid side chains. After doing some searching it seems that tryptophan mainly participates in nonpolar interactions, whereas hydrogen bonding plays a minor role. For the purposes of this class, we will categorize tryptophan as just a nonpolar interactor.

  • 09/19/2022 (Mon): Protein Folding and Thermodynamics - In-Person
    • Worksheet
    • Worksheet answer key

    Thermodynamics is a topic which students tend to have a difficult time understanding in this course. My advice for dealing with questions of thermodynamics? Understand the components you are working with. At least in biochemistry, we are primarily concerned with whether a system forms or breaks bonds (change in dH) or whether entropy is increased or decreased with a process (change in dS). In biological systems, the temperature hardly ever changes, so when considering the free energy change of a reaction, enthalpy and entropy changes are the ones which matter. Always ask: is this process forming or breaking bonds? Will there be an increase or a decrease in the entropy of the system following this process? Understanding these relationships will be key to helping you understand thermodynamics.
