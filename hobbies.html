Programming

Full Disclosure: I am not at all an expert at programming. In fact, this whole website is an experiment for me to practice my HTML and CSS skills. However, over the course of my college career, I've become convinced that as a researcher, being able to program is an invaluable tool. Sitting down and learning to code, learning a few tricks in Python, Java or even HTML and CSS ⁠— whatever language you want ⁠— can save a ton of time doing menial computer tasks in the future. You absolutely won't regret it, I promise!

A good example of how programming saved me valuable time was when I was still working on metabolic research at UMD. In June of 2021, I frequently worked with gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS) analytical methods, specifically, with the bundled Agilent MassHunter Quantitative Analysis software which exports data of intermediary metabolites. OK, so if you've never heard of that before, you may be thinking to yourself, what the heck is he talking about? Essentially, the software for our GC-MS machine spit out an ocean of data across dozens of metabolites from our study, each with two or more isotopes to track and dozens of samples of different groups to yield thousands of mass spectrometer peaks. Organizing these spreadsheets into a readable form through Excel was a tedious and error-prone hell which often took over an hour or two to complete. However, after noticing patterns in the organization of the data and realizing that I had some background in Java, I developed a simple program over my spring break to organize the raw data into a readable form. After a few days of work on my project (and almost losing all of my progess - "git reset -hard" is a dangerous command), what was a laborious and fault-prone hour or two hour long process was quickly reduced to mere seconds of work.

Since then, I have found creative ways to apply programming to all sorts of tasks in research, such as developing a script to organize raw RT-PCR data and analyze it using the Livak method to yield gene expression levels, or a program which tracks specific indices of amino acid sequences and returns the corresponding codon sequeunce for that residue to design mutant proteins.

I think the best part about integrating programming into one's research is the respect you earn from your colleagues. Especially in the fields of biotechnology and clinical research, skills in computer science are highly sought after. While being a computer scientist at a big tech company is common, having programming skills in other disciplines will surely make you stand out as an asset. I recall after I completed the RT-PCR and GC-MS programs for analyzing huge data sets, many of my colleagues that had worked with the data by hand before I developed the program expressed their appreciation for saving valuable time by automating the tedious task. For many of them, the process was almost magical. People's reaction to my hidden skill is part of the reason why I like programming, not only as a research tool, but as a hobby.

Language Acquisition

Why is learning a language important? Well, in my experience, one of the most challenging adversities barring immigrants from social mobility is the language barrier. When my parents initially immigrated to the United States from China, work was scarce as many employers in rural Minnesota did not want to hire non-native English speakers. The silent discrimination our family faced forced us to constantly move across state lines in search of work, and my parents often felt alienated from American society, believing that they did not belong in this country. The linguistic prejudice my family endured is the primary reason why I developed an interest in language learning. I wanted to assure that no one, immigrant or otherwise, would feel excluded by a narrow-minded few. Seeking to uphold my principles and test my communication skills, I committed myself to community involvement. Though it has been a while, the summer of my sophomore year in high school I volunteered as an interpreter for the International Offices at Montgomery County Public Schools. Facilitating the vaccination process for newly immigrated families, I gave directions and provided information in Chinese and French. The experience was transformative, especially as someone who empathized with the adversities immigrant families faced. Offering guidance was more than just a means of breaking down the language barrier. In retrospect, my more profound goal was to communicate that sense of belongingness which was absent in my immigration journey. All languages are beautiful in their own way.

Though I was born in the US, English was technically not my first language. Soon after I was born, I lived in China with my mother and grandparents for around 2 years. My first word was actually a Chinese word, 灯 (deng(1)), meaning light! As was common in many traditional Chinese households, I memorized ancient Chinese poems (唐诗 tang(2) shi(1)) during my time in China (some of which I still remember today), and became proficient in Chinese before I even knew basic English. Early in high school, I learned Latin in class, which I used to develop skills in two romance languages, French and Spanish. I actually became conversational in French at a point (after around 3 months of self-studying), but eventually forgot much of it as school began to get busier and I had less time to practice. Sometime around my high school years I also picked up the ability to read Cyrillic and Hangul, though I have not properly learned Russian and Korean themselves. Around my senior year of high school I also began to pick up Japanese, since my father had learned it before and because I found the culture of Japan to be interesting. Additionally, the challenge of learning a language as hard as Japanese was also part of the appeal to me (call me a masochist). As of 2022 I am conversational in Japanese, and am able to read, write, and speak at a basic level.
